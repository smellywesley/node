# NODE Private Pilot Beneficiary Proposal

Updated: 2026-07-05
Status: pre-sales proposal before selling 3-5 private pilots

## Executive Decision

Do not make the website the main sales motion yet. Keep the landing page as a credibility and booking surface, but sell through founder-led proof conversations.

NODE should first target teams with one urgent problem:

> AI coding agents are starting to touch real repositories, but the team cannot prove what the agent was allowed to do, what it was blocked from doing, who approved it, what it cost, and what evidence can be exported afterward.

The first paid pilots should not be sold as a broad AI operating system, hosted SaaS platform, or model-credit product. They should be sold as a private/local control proof for one real repository-shaped workflow.

## Who Benefits Most

### 1. Platform Engineering And DevEx Teams

Best fit when they are already rolling out Cursor, Claude Code, Copilot agents, Devin-style workflows, Codex-style coding agents, or internal repo agents.

Why they benefit:

- They own developer productivity but inherit the operational risk.
- They need a repeatable way to say which paths, tools, and actions are allowed.
- They need evidence before expanding agent usage across more repositories.
- They can evaluate a local/private proof without requiring a full hosted enterprise platform.

Pilot hook:

> Let your coding agent work inside one repo, but make every consequential action pass through policy, approval, cost, and audit.

Best first workflow:

- Backend-only fix where frontend, infra, or secrets paths must be denied.
- Test-running workflow where the agent can propose a branch/diff but cannot freely write outside scope.
- Internal tool maintenance task with a known safe path and one intentionally forbidden path.

### 2. Security Engineering And AppSec Teams

Best fit when security is being asked to approve AI coding tools but has no concrete audit evidence.

Why they benefit:

- They do not want to trust prompt instructions as a security boundary.
- They care about file scope, secrets, network access, approval gates, and auditability.
- They need an artifact they can review after the run, not just a demo screenshot.
- They can become the internal champion if NODE answers a real policy question.

Pilot hook:

> Do not ask the model to behave. Put the agent inside a controlled run and export the evidence.

Best first workflow:

- "Agent may edit backend test files, but cannot touch frontend, deployment, secrets, or config."
- "Agent must pause for human approval before any write outside a predeclared safe path."
- "Agent run must produce an audit bundle suitable for internal review."

### 3. AI Governance, Risk, And Compliance Teams

Best fit when the organization is actively discussing AI governance and needs proof around autonomous software work.

Why they benefit:

- They need evidence that governance exists before AI agents scale.
- They care about accountability, approval, retention, and repeatability.
- They need simple language for leadership: what happened, who approved it, what was denied, what did it cost, and what proof exists.

Pilot hook:

> Convert a risky AI-agent run into a reviewable control record.

Best first workflow:

- A policy review demo where the agent attempts allowed and denied actions.
- A redacted audit-bundle review with security/compliance stakeholders.
- A governance-readiness report after one controlled local run.

### 4. CTOs Or Engineering Leaders At AI-Native Startups

Best fit when the team is moving fast with agents but is beginning to feel the pain of uncontrolled automation.

Why they benefit:

- They already believe agents are useful.
- They need a lightweight control layer before process complexity explodes.
- They can pay for a fast pilot if the proof is concrete and founder-led.

Pilot hook:

> Keep agent speed, but add the control record you will need once the team grows.

Best first workflow:

- One production-adjacent repository task.
- One explicit cost cap.
- One approval gate.
- One exported run summary they can show internally.

## Who To Avoid For The First 3-5 Pilots

Avoid these buyers for now:

- Solo hobbyists who only want a smarter chatbot.
- Nontechnical buyers who want generic automation.
- Enterprises that require hosted SSO, full RBAC, procurement, and external security review before any private proof.
- Teams that want NODE to supply managed model credits.
- Buyers who cannot bring one repository-shaped workflow.
- Buyers who want broad custom consulting instead of one controlled run proof.

These segments may matter later, but they will slow down the first evidence cycle.

## Pilot Offer

Sell one narrow proof:

**NODE Pro Private Pilot**

- Price: S$750 for a founder-led entry private proof, with higher pricing for deeper integration after scope review.
- Duration: 1-2 weeks for the first paid proof, 2-4 weeks for a deeper design-partner pilot.
- Scope: one repo-shaped workflow, one policy boundary, one approval gate, one cost cap, one audit bundle.
- Deployment: local/private only.
- Model posture: BYOK.
- Output:
  - proof packet;
  - denied action evidence;
  - approved action evidence;
  - cost/usage evidence;
  - redacted audit bundle;
  - next-step recommendation.

Do not promise:

- hosted multi-tenant SaaS;
- SSO/RBAC;
- managed model credits;
- production enterprise control plane;
- broad custom agent marketplace.

## Discovery Questions

Ask these before selling:

1. Which coding agents are already touching repositories?
2. What action would be unacceptable if an agent did it without approval?
3. Which files, tools, or secrets should agents never touch?
4. Who needs to approve risky agent actions?
5. What proof would satisfy security, platform, or leadership?
6. Can you run a local/private Docker-based proof with your own model keys?
7. What would make a paid pilot worth it inside 30 days?

## Sales Sequence Before The 3-5 Pilots

1. Build a list of 25 target teams, not random companies.
2. Score each team with the existing pilot scorecard.
3. Send a narrow message about controlled coding-agent runs, not "AI OS."
4. Book a 25-minute fit check.
5. Ask for one repository-shaped workflow.
6. Run or replay the 5-minute proof packet.
7. Send the buyer-specific pilot proposal.
8. Ask for payment or invoice approval before custom integration.
9. Capture the learning after every call.
10. Only count a lead as real when they have a workflow, owner, proof need, and timeline.

## Success Criteria

The proposal is working if the first conversations produce:

- named owner;
- real coding-agent workflow;
- clear risky action;
- required audit evidence;
- local/private proof acceptance;
- willingness to pay for a pilot;
- next paid feature chosen from evidence.

Do not count compliments. Count only workflows, owners, payment willingness, and proof needs.

## Recommendation

The first 3-5 pilots should focus on platform, DevEx, AppSec, and security engineering teams that already permit or are actively evaluating AI coding agents against repositories.

The offer should be:

> Bring one risky coding-agent workflow. NODE will show the request, policy gate, sandbox, cost meter, approval, replay, and audit bundle around that run.

That is the wedge. Everything else, including hosted backend, broader pricing, and enterprise platform claims, comes after those pilots prove repeated buyer pain.
